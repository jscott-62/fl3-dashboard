## Email Body

Hey there,

I want to share something that changed how I think about the entire financial advice industry.

It starts with one question: **How does your financial advisor get paid?**

Most charge 1% of your total assets. Every year. Whether the market goes up, down, or sideways.

Think about what that incentive creates.

Their job isn't to grow your wealth as fast as possible. Their job is to keep your money where it is. Because the longer it stays, the more they earn.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's just a business model. And once you see it, you can't unsee it.

### The Three Moves

I've been studying how the traditional advice industry keeps people locked in, and it comes down to three predictable moves:

**Move #1: The Complexity Shield.**
They make investing sound impossibly complicated. Asset allocation models. Monte Carlo simulations. Tax-loss harvesting. These are real concepts, but they're often deployed to make you feel like you can't do this without them.

If you're someone who likes to research things thoroughly before acting (I know a lot of you are), this one is especially effective. They give you just enough complexity to keep you researching forever. Just enough to prevent you from ever looking elsewhere.

**Move #2: The Risk Scare.**
Anything outside their recommended portfolio is "too risky." They'll show you horror stories about alternatives.

But here's what they'll never show you: the worst-case scenario of their own advice. Which is retiring with half of what you need because 7% average returns couldn't outrun real inflation over 30 years.

**Move #3: The Time Delay.**
"You still have time." "Let's revisit next quarter." "The market will recover."

Every delay benefits them (more fees) and hurts you (less time for your money to work). If you're 50 and the math isn't adding up, you don't have the luxury of "let's wait and see."

### The Real Math

Here's what this looks like in numbers:

$300,000 in a managed account. 7% annual return. 1.5% total fees.

Your real return: 5.5%.

Now factor in actual inflation. Not the government number. The real cost-of-living increase you feel at the store and the pump. Conservative estimates put it at 7-10%.

At 5.5% returns and 8% real inflation, your purchasing power drops by 2.5% every year.

You're not building wealth. You're losing it slowly enough that the quarterly statement still looks green.

### What I'd Do

I'm not saying fire your advisor tomorrow. But I am saying this:

At your next meeting, ask one question: **"What are the incentives behind this recommendation?"**

Watch how they respond. That response will tell you everything you need to know.
