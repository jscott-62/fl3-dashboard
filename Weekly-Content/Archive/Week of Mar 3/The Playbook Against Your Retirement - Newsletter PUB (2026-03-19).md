## Email Body

I need to show you something.

Last week I sat down and mapped out exactly how the traditional financial advice industry works. Not the theory. The actual mechanics. How the money flows, who benefits, and why the advice never seems to change no matter how much the world does.

What I found wasn't sinister. It was worse than that. It was structural.

### The Incentive Problem

Your financial advisor charges about 1% of your total assets. Every year. Whether the market goes up, down, or sideways.

That means their incentive isn't to grow your wealth as fast as possible. Their incentive is to keep your money where it is. Because the longer it stays, the more they earn.

Here's the thing. **They're not bad people.** Most advisors genuinely believe they're helping you. They went through the same training, passed the same certifications, and learned the same playbook everyone else in the industry uses.

The problem isn't the person across the table. It's the playbook they're running. And that playbook was written in the 1970s for a world with 4% inflation and pension plans.

### The Three Moves

Once you see the incentive problem, you start noticing three moves the industry uses over and over:

**Move #1: The Complexity Shield.** They make investing sound impossibly complicated. Asset allocation, Monte Carlo simulations, tax-loss harvesting. Real concepts, but often deployed to make you feel like you can't do this without them. If you're someone who likes to research things thoroughly before acting (I know a lot of you are), this one is especially effective. They give you just enough complexity to keep you in research mode forever.

**Move #2: The Risk Scare.** Anything outside their recommended portfolio is "too risky." They'll show you horror stories. But here's what they'll never show you: the worst-case scenario of their own advice. Which is retiring with half of what you need because 7% returns couldn't keep up with real inflation.

**Move #3: The Time Delay.** "You still have time." "Let's revisit next quarter." Every delay benefits them (more fees) and hurts you (less time). If you're 50+ and the math isn't adding up, "let's wait and see" isn't patience. It's expensive.

### The Math That Should Make You Angry

$300,000 in a managed account. 7% annual return. 1.5% total fees. Real return: 5.5%.

Now factor in real inflation. Not the government number. The actual cost-of-living increase you feel at the store. Conservative estimates: 7 to 10%.

At 5.5% returns and 8% real inflation, your purchasing power drops roughly 2.5% every year. The quarterly statement still looks green. But you're losing ground.

That's not a plan. That's a slow bleed disguised as progress.

### What I'd Do

I'm not saying fire your advisor tomorrow. They're operating inside a system that trained them to think this way.

But at your next meeting, ask one question:

**"What are the incentives behind this recommendation?"**

Watch how they respond. That response tells you everything about whether the advice is designed for your benefit or for theirs.
