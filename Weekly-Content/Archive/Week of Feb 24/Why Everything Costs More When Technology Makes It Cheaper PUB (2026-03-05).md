 The Question Nobody's Asking 

Here's something that should bother you.

Your first smartphone cost a fortune. Today, a phone ten times more powerful costs half as much. Computing power that used to fill a warehouse now fits in your pocket. Software that once cost thousands is free. Storage, manufacturing, logistics. All cheaper. All faster. All better.

Technology has been driving costs down for decades.

So why does everything feel more expensive?

Why are groceries up? Why is housing through the roof? Why does it take two incomes to live the life one income used to cover?

If you're in your 40s, 50s, or 60s, you've watched this happen in real time. You remember when a family could get by. You've felt the squeeze tighten year after year. And you've been told, over and over, that inflation is just "normal."

It's not.

And once you understand why, you'll never look at your money the same way again.

## What Jeff Booth Figured Out (That Most Economists Won't Say)

Jeff Booth is a tech entrepreneur and author who spent years asking a question that should be obvious but almost nobody asks.

If technology makes things cheaper, and technology is accelerating, why aren't prices falling?

His answer is simple. And uncomfortable.

**Prices aren't rising because things are scarce. Prices are rising because your money is.**

Here's what he means.

Technology is deflationary. It naturally pushes prices down. That's not theory. You've lived it. Your phone, your streaming service, your ability to video call someone across the world for free. All of that is technology doing what technology does. Making things cheaper and more abundant.

But our financial system can't survive falling prices.

Why? Because the entire system runs on debt. And debt only works if money loses value over time. That's inflation. It's not a natural law of economics. It's a policy choice. Governments and central banks engineer inflation because without it, the debt machine breaks down.

So instead of letting technology pass those savings to you, the system prints more money, expands credit, and pushes prices back up.

You're caught in the middle of two forces pulling in opposite directions.

Technology wants to make your life cheaper. The money system needs to make it more expensive.

Guess which one wins.

## Why You Were Never Taught This

If this feels like new information, it's not your fault.

You grew up hearing that 2% inflation is healthy. That markets must always go up. That prices rising is just how economies work. Every textbook, every news anchor, every financial advisor reinforced the same story.

And here's the thing. They weren't lying. They were just repeating what they were taught, too.

The effects are indirect and hard to see. Technology makes your phone cheaper, sure. But you don't connect that to macroeconomic forces. You see gas prices and grocery bills and assume that's just life.

Most economic models were built in an era where growth was tied to physical labor and physical capital. Not exponential technological change. The old models don't account for the world we actually live in.

And there's an emotional layer, too. Mainstream economics treats deflation (falling prices) as something dangerous. A threat. Something to be feared. So people are taught to believe that rising prices protect them.

They don't.

Rising prices protect the system. Not you.

## The Part That Changes Everything

Here's where it gets real.

Booth's core insight isn't just an academic observation. It's a framework for understanding why you feel stuck. Why saving money in a bank account feels pointless. Why your purchasing power keeps shrinking even when you do everything "right."

**Deflation from technology is a gift. Debt turns that gift into a crisis. Inflation is the cover story.**

Let that sink in.

Technology should be making your retirement easier to fund, your savings more powerful, your cost of living lower. Every year, productivity gains should be flowing directly to you. Better goods. Lower prices. More freedom.

Instead, those gains get absorbed by a system that needs to keep inflating.

Your purchasing power erodes by design. Not by accident. By design.

That's why your 401(k) needs to return 7% just to keep up. That's why "safe" investments feel like they're going backward. That's why the traditional advice to park your money in bonds and wait feels like a trap.

Because it is one.

## What This Means for Your Retirement

If you're behind on retirement savings (and statistically, most people over 45 are), this isn't just interesting theory. This is the lens that makes everything else make sense.

Traditional financial advice told you to save steadily, invest conservatively, and trust the system. That advice assumed a world where inflation was a mild, manageable force. A gentle tailwind.

But inflation isn't gentle. It's accelerating. And the tools they gave you weren't built for this environment.

Here's what Booth's framework reveals about your situation.

**The system is not broken.** It's working exactly as designed. It's designed to transfer wealth from savers to debtors. From people who hold cash to people who hold assets. From people who follow the rules to people who understand how the game actually works.

**Deflation is not the villain.** Falling prices from technology and productivity are good for everyone. They increase abundance. They make life more affordable. The fear of deflation was manufactured to protect institutions that can't function without inflation.

**Inflation is not protecting you.** It's protecting the system. Every dollar you hold loses purchasing power on purpose. Not because the economy needs it. Because the debt load requires it.

## The Two Forces (A Simple Way to See It)

Think of it this way.

On one side, you have technology. Automation, software, AI, efficiency. All of it pushing costs down. Making goods cheaper. Making abundance possible.

On the other side, you have the monetary system. Debt, money printing, credit expansion. All of it pushing prices up. Making your dollar worth less every year.

These two forces are in direct conflict.

And most people only ever learned about one side. The monetary side. The side that says inflation is normal and necessary.

Nobody taught you about the other side. The side that says technology should be making your life cheaper every single year.

Once you see both sides, you can't unsee it.

## So What Do You Actually Do?

This isn't about doom and gloom. Far from it.

Once you understand the game, you can stop playing by the old rules. You can stop blaming yourself for feeling behind. You can stop trusting advice that was designed for a world that no longer exists.

And you can start asking better questions.

Questions like: If my money loses value by design, where should I store it? If technology is deflationary and the system is inflationary, which side do I want to be on? If traditional savings vehicles can't outrun inflation, what can?

These are the questions that change everything. Not because the answers are simple. But because the old answers were wrong.

You're not behind because you made bad decisions. You're behind because the rules changed and nobody told you.

The system kept inflating. Your purchasing power kept shrinking. And the advice you were given assumed none of that would happen.

Now you know better. And knowing is the first step toward building something different.
