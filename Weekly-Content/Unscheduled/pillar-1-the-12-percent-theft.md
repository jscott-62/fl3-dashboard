### Part B: The Day They Changed the Rules (5:00 - 7:30)

Let me show you something.

[GRAPHIC: M2 Money Supply chart from 1970 to present]

This is the M2 money supply. It measures how much money exists in the U.S. economy. Savings accounts, checking accounts, money market funds. The actual pool of dollars in circulation.

Look at this chart. For decades, it grew slowly. Steadily. The way things are supposed to work.

And then look at what happened.

[GRAPHIC: Highlight the inflection points, particularly 2008 and 2020]

The Federal Reserve made a decision. Actually, they made a series of decisions. To print money at a pace that has no historical precedent. Trillions of dollars. Created from nothing.

Now, here's the part nobody talks about.

When you print more money, each dollar you already have buys less. That's not controversial. That's not a conspiracy theory. That's basic economics.

But the benefits of printing money don't go to you. They go to people who borrow money. Banks. Corporations. The government. Debtors win when money is created. Savers lose.

[GRAPHIC: Simple split screen, "Winners: Borrowers, Banks, Government" vs. "Losers: Savers, Retirees, Fixed-income workers"]

Does it make sense that policies designed to help debtors would hurt savers?

Think about that for a second.

The Federal Reserve's own stated goal is 2% inflation per year. Their goal is to make your money worth less. Every single year. On purpose.

You weren't crazy for feeling like the game was rigged. The game WAS rigged. Just not the way you expected.

[GRAPHIC: Animated counter showing the growth of the Federal Reserve's balance sheet]

Where did that money go?

It went into financial assets. Stocks. Bonds. Real estate. It inflated the prices of things that wealthy people own. If you owned a house in 2010, great. Your house went up. If you owned stocks, great. The market went up.

But if you were a saver? If you were putting cash into a savings account or a conservative portfolio? You got left behind. Because the thing that went up the most was the cost of everything you need.

[GRAPHIC: Asset prices up 200-300% vs. Wages up 30-40% since 2008]

Your financial advisor tells you that you're getting 7 to 10 percent returns. Let's be generous. Let's say 10.

Not the official inflation number. The REAL one.

[GRAPHIC: Two columns building, "Official CPI Inflation: 3.2%" on the left]

The Consumer Price Index says inflation is around 3 percent. That's the number your advisor uses. That's the number the government reports.

But the CPI is an average. It includes TVs getting cheaper. Software getting faster. Things you don't need to survive.

[GRAPHIC: Right column builds, "What you ACTUALLY pay: Housing 6-8%, Healthcare 7-10%, Food 5-7%, Education 6-8%, Insurance 8-12%"]

The stuff that actually matters for retirement. The stuff you can't stop buying. Housing. Healthcare. Food. Insurance.

That's running 6 to 10 percent. Some categories higher.

This is the 12% number nobody talks about.

[GRAPHIC: Animated equation building step by step: "10% market return - 2% fees - 8% real inflation = 0% ACTUAL growth"]

Your 10 percent return. Minus the 1.5 to 2 percent you're paying in fees (and yes, you're paying them, even if you never see a bill). Minus the 6 to 8 percent your cost of living is actually rising.

What's left?

Zero to 2 percent. Real growth.

Your money isn't failing to grow. It's being stolen. Not by some villain. By a system that was designed for a world where inflation ran at 2 percent and people retired at 62 with a pension.

That world doesn't exist anymore.

But I need to show you something that changed the way I think about risk entirely.

[GRAPHIC: Two boxes side by side, "Visible Risk" and "Invisible Risk"]

There are two types of risk. And most people only see one of them.

Visible risk is the kind you already know about. Market drops. Volatility. That red number on your screen. It's scary because you can see it.

Invisible risk is the kind we've been talking about. The slow, silent erosion of your purchasing power. Year after year. It doesn't send you a notification. It doesn't make the news. But it destroys your retirement just as effectively.

The difference? Visible risk recovers. Markets crash and then they come back. Every single time in history. Invisible risk doesn't recover. Once your purchasing power is gone, it's gone.

You've been driving a minivan. It's reliable. It's safe. It gets decent gas mileage. Nothing wrong with a minivan.

Unless you're in a race.

You don't need a better driver. You don't need to press the gas harder. You need a different vehicle.

That's the situation most people over 50 are in. They're not behind because they drove badly. They're behind because they're in the wrong vehicle.

### Part B: Preservation Assets vs. Catch-Up Assets (24:00 - 26:00)

Here's a framework that simplified everything for me.

[GRAPHIC: Two columns, "Preservation Assets" and "Catch-Up Assets"]

Preservation assets are designed to protect wealth you've already built. Think bonds. Treasury bills. High-yield savings accounts. Stable value funds.

These are fine if you're 70 and you've already hit your number. You're just trying to protect what you have.

But if you're 50-something and you're behind? Preservation assets are the minivan. They were never designed for catching up. They were designed for people who already won the race.

Catch-up assets are different. They carry more visible risk. They're volatile. But they have the potential to outpace inflation by a significant margin.

[GRAPHIC: Historical comparison chart showing different asset class returns over 10-year periods, net of real inflation]

The math difference is enormous. At 2 percent real growth, your $200,000 becomes $243,000 in 10 years. At 15 percent real growth, that same $200,000 becomes $808,000.

Same starting point. Same timeline. Different vehicle.

I'm not telling you which specific assets to buy. That's a different conversation. What I'm telling you is that the vehicle matters more than the effort. It matters more than the time. It matters more than discipline or sacrifice or starting early enough.

Does the right-tool-for-the-job principle make sense?

### Part C: Why Your 401(k) Was Designed for 25-Year-Olds (26:00 - 27:00)

One more thing.

The 401(k) was created in 1978. It was designed for people starting their careers in their 20s who would invest for 35 to 40 years and let compound interest do the heavy lifting.

[GRAPHIC: Timeline showing 401(k) creation in 1978 and the assumptions baked into it]

It was designed for a world of low inflation, long careers, and company pensions as a safety net.

None of those assumptions hold anymore.

If you're 55 and relying on a 401(k) as your primary vehicle, you're using a tool that was never designed for your situation. It's not that the tool is broken. It's that it was built for a completely different job.

You don't have a retirement savings problem. You have a wrong vehicle problem.
