### Part B: How to Calculate Your Personal Inflation Theft (5:00 - 8:30)

Remember the 12 percent concept from the first video? Real inflation (the stuff you actually buy) running at 6 to 10 percent per year while the CPI says 3 percent?

Let's apply that to YOUR money.

[GRAPHIC: Calculator building step by step with example numbers]

Let's use an example first. We'll call her Diane. She's 55, has $250,000 saved, and wants to retire at 67.

Step one. What does her $250,000 actually buy?

Her portfolio has been earning about 8 percent nominal returns. Her advisor is happy. The statements look good.

But after fees (let's say 1.5 percent) and real inflation (let's say 7 percent), her real return is negative 0.5 percent per year.

[GRAPHIC: Animated equation: 8% return - 1.5% fees - 7% real inflation = -0.5% real return]

She's not growing. She's shrinking. By half a percent per year in real purchasing power.

Step two. What has she already lost?

If Diane has been investing for 20 years at a negative 0.5 percent real return, her $250,000 looks great on paper. But in purchasing power terms? It's been eroding.

Let's compare what she thinks she has versus what it actually buys.

[GRAPHIC: Two bars, "Statement Value: $250,000" vs. "Real Purchasing Power: ~$180,000 to $200,000 in today's terms"]

That's $50,000 to $70,000 in invisible theft. Already gone. Slowly, over 20 years, while her statements looked perfectly fine.

Step three. Now apply this to YOUR numbers.

[GRAPHIC: Blank calculator fields for viewer to fill in]

Take your current savings. Subtract 1 to 2 percent for fees. Subtract 6 to 8 percent for real inflation. What's your actual real return?

If the answer is near zero or negative, you're in the same boat as Diane. Your savings aren't growing in real terms. They're treading water. Or sinking.

Write that number down. That's the truth behind your statements.

## SECTION 2: YOUR FREEDOM NUMBER (15:00 - 25:00)

### Part A: Calculating Your Personal Freedom Number (15:00 - 18:30)

Your Freedom Number is the specific amount you need to retire on your terms. Not someone else's terms. Yours.

Here's how we calculate it.

[GRAPHIC: Step-by-step Freedom Number calculator]

Step one. Your monthly expenses in retirement.

Not your current expenses. Your retirement expenses. Think about what you need to cover: housing, healthcare, food, transportation, insurance, and whatever gives you the quality of life you want.

For most people, this is 70 to 85 percent of their current spending. Some people spend more in early retirement (travel, hobbies) and less later. Pick a number that feels honest.

Let's say Diane needs $5,000 per month. That's $60,000 per year.

Step two. Social Security offset.

At 67, Diane estimates she'll receive about $2,200 per month from Social Security. That's $26,400 per year.

So her annual gap is $60,000 minus $26,400 = $33,600 per year that needs to come from her savings and investments.

Step three. The 4 percent rule (with a reality check).

The traditional advice says you can safely withdraw 4 percent of your portfolio per year. So to generate $33,600 per year, Diane needs $33,600 divided by 0.04 = $840,000.

That's her Freedom Number. $840,000.

[GRAPHIC: "$840,000" displayed prominently]

But here's the reality check. The 4 percent rule was designed for an era of 3 percent inflation. In a world of 6 to 8 percent real inflation, 4 percent might be too aggressive.

A more conservative number: 3 percent withdrawal rate. Which means Diane needs $33,600 divided by 0.03 = $1,120,000.

Let's split the difference and call her Freedom Number $1,000,000. That gives her a comfortable buffer.

Now calculate yours.

[GRAPHIC: Viewer calculator template]

Your monthly retirement expenses, minus estimated Social Security, multiplied by 12, divided by 0.035 (a moderate withdrawal rate).

That's your Freedom Number. Write it down.

### Part C: The Gap, And How to Close It (22:00 - 25:00)

Let me show you what closing the gap actually requires.

[GRAPHIC: Sensitivity analysis table]

For Diane, to reach $1,000,000 in 12 years starting from $250,000:

At 2 percent real return: She needs to contribute $4,100 per month. Not realistic.
At 5 percent real return: She needs $2,800 per month. Tough but possible.
At 10 percent real return: She needs $1,200 per month. Doable.
At 15 percent real return: She needs $500 per month. Already doing it.

[GRAPHIC: The required monthly contribution dropping as the return rate increases]

Look at that. At 2 percent real return, she'd need to find $4,100 per month. That's impossible for most people.

But at 15 percent real return, she's already there. The $500 she's currently contributing is enough. She just needs to move it to the right vehicle.

That's the power of vehicle selection. It turns an impossible gap into a manageable one.

Now look at YOUR gap. What return rate would close it with your current contributions? What if you increased slightly?

The math might surprise you. Because the difference between "impossible" and "I can actually do this" often isn't more money. It's a better vehicle.

## SECTION 3: VEHICLE SELECTION CONTEXT (25:00 - 32:00)

### Part A: Making It Personal (25:00 - 28:00)

Now that you see YOUR gap, let's talk about what it means for your vehicle selection.

[GRAPHIC: Asset class comparison chart, personalized to different gap sizes]

If your gap is small (under $200,000), you might be able to close it with moderate adjustments. Moving from preservation assets to a blend of growth and catch-up assets.

If your gap is medium ($200,000 to $500,000), you need meaningful vehicle change. The math requires assets with double-digit real returns. This is where catch-up assets become necessary, not optional.

If your gap is large (over $500,000), the traditional path can't get you there. Period. Not in 10 to 15 years. The only way to close a large gap in a short timeline is a fundamentally different vehicle.

I'm not telling you which specific vehicle to choose. That depends on your situation, your risk tolerance, and your timeline.

But I am telling you this: if your gap is medium to large, the "safe" path isn't safe. It's a guarantee of failure. The math is absolute.

### Part B: Given YOUR Timeline and YOUR Gap (28:00 - 30:00)

Here's the framework.

[GRAPHIC: Decision matrix]

Your timeline combined with your gap determines your vehicle.

Long timeline (15+ years) plus small gap: Traditional assets might still work. Slow and steady could be enough.

Long timeline plus large gap: You need catch-up assets for at least a portion of your portfolio. The math requires it.

Short timeline (under 10 years) plus small gap: A blend could work. Some growth assets to close the gap, some preservation to protect what you have.

Short timeline plus large gap: You need the highest-performing vehicles available. This is where asset selection becomes the single most important decision you'll make.

Given YOUR timeline and YOUR gap, here's what the math requires.

[GRAPHIC: Personal decision template]

If the math says you need 15 percent real returns to close your gap, then assets that deliver 2 percent real returns aren't a "safe" choice. They're a guaranteed shortfall.

This isn't about being reckless. It's about matching your vehicle to your destination. A minivan won't win a race. A preservation asset won't close a catch-up gap.
