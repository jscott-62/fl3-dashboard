## SECTION 1: The Math You Were Never Shown (0:45 - 4:15)

Here's what they told you.

Save 10 to 15 percent of your income. Put it in a 401(k). Buy some index funds. Get your 7 to 10 percent average annual return. Do that for 30 or 40 years. Retire with dignity.

And you did it. Maybe not perfectly, but you did it. You contributed. You stayed the course. You didn't panic sell (or maybe you did once, in 2008, and you've been kicking yourself ever since).

The point is, you tried. You followed the playbook.

So why does your retirement account look like it belongs to someone 20 years younger?

[GRAPHIC: Bar chart showing "Expected retirement savings at age 55" vs. "Actual average retirement savings at age 55" with sources (Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances)]

The median retirement savings for Americans aged 55 to 64 is around $185,000. That's the median. Half of all people in that age group have less.

Now here's where the math gets uncomfortable.

[GRAPHIC: Simple calculator showing "$185,000 growing at 7% for 10 years = $363,000"]

Even if you don't touch that money for another 10 years, and it grows at the historical average, you're looking at maybe $360,000.

Financial planners say you need $1.5 to $2 million to retire comfortably. Some say more.

So where did the other $1.2 million go?

That's the question nobody wants to answer. Because the answer isn't "you didn't save enough." The answer is a number. A very specific number that's been working against you your entire investing life.

---

## SECTION 2: The 12% Number (4:15 - 8:30)

The number is 12.

Not 12 percent returns. Twelve percent loss. Every single year.

Here's what I mean.

Your financial advisor tells you that you're getting 7 to 10 percent returns on your portfolio. Let's be generous and say 10.

[GRAPHIC: Simple equation building step by step]

But here's what they don't subtract:

Inflation. The real kind. Not the government's version. The actual increase in the cost of the things you need. Housing. Healthcare. Food. Education.

[GRAPHIC: Side by side: "Official CPI Inflation: 3.2%" vs. "Real cost increases you experience: Housing up 6-8%, Healthcare up 7-10%, Food up 5-7%, Education up 6-8%"]

The official Consumer Price Index says inflation is around 3 percent. But that number is an average across all goods and services. It includes TVs getting cheaper and software getting faster.

The stuff that actually matters for retirement? The stuff you can't avoid buying? That's running 6 to 10 percent. Some years higher.

So let's do the real math.

[GRAPHIC: Animated equation: "10% market return - 2% fees - 6-8% real inflation = 0 to 2% ACTUAL growth"]

Your 10 percent return. Minus the 1.5 to 2 percent you're paying in fees (and yes, you're paying them, even if you don't see a bill). Minus the 6 to 8 percent your cost of living is actually rising.

What's left? Zero to 2 percent. Real growth.

That's it. That's the number. Your money isn't failing to grow. It's actively being stolen. Not by some villain. By a system that was designed for a world where inflation ran at 2 percent and people retired at 62 with a pension.

That world doesn't exist anymore.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "Your money isn't failing to grow. It's being stolen by invisible inflation."]

This is why you're behind. This is why you feel like no matter how much you save, it's never enough. Because at 0 to 2 percent real growth, you would need to save an almost impossible amount to hit your retirement target.

The math. Doesn't. Work.

And it's not your fault.

## SECTION 3: Why Your Advisor Has Never Shown You This (8:30 - 11:15)

So here's the obvious question. If this is just basic math, why hasn't your financial advisor sat you down and shown you this?

I'll give you three reasons.

Number one. Their entire business model depends on you staying in the system.

[GRAPHIC: Simple flow chart showing "Your money → mutual funds → management fees → advisor fees → your growth (what's left)"]

Your advisor gets paid whether your money grows or not. Those fees? They come off the top. Every year. Rain or shine. Bear market or bull market. They eat first.

Now I'm not saying your advisor is a bad person. Most of them genuinely believe in what they're selling. They were trained in the same system you were raised in. Save, diversify, be patient.

But here's the thing. They can't tell you the math doesn't work because that would mean admitting the very product they sell isn't fit for purpose. That's like a car salesman telling you the engine doesn't work, but the leather seats are really nice.

Number two. They don't have anything better to offer you.

Traditional financial planning has a very limited menu. Stocks. Bonds. Mutual funds. ETFs. Maybe some real estate. That's the sandbox they play in.

If the math doesn't work inside that sandbox, they don't have the authority or the framework to point you somewhere else. They're licensed to sell what they're licensed to sell.

Number three. They're scared too.

[GRAPHIC: Quote text "The hardest truth to accept is the one that makes your own expertise irrelevant."]

Think about it. If your advisor has spent 20 years building expertise in traditional portfolio management, and someone comes along and says "that whole thing doesn't work anymore," that's terrifying. For them. Their identity is wrapped up in this.

So they don't show you the 12 percent number. They don't subtract real inflation. They show you the nominal returns and tell you to stay the course.

And meanwhile, your purchasing power gets a little smaller every year.

## SECTION 4: What This Actually Means for You (11:15 - 13:30)

Let me make this personal. Because I know numbers on a screen can feel abstract.

Let's say you're 55. You've got $200,000 saved. You want to retire at 67. That gives you 12 years.

[GRAPHIC: Scenario calculator showing the following three paths]

Path one. Traditional advice. Keep doing what you're doing. 7 percent nominal returns, minus fees, minus real inflation. Your $200,000 becomes roughly $240,000 to $280,000 in real purchasing power. In 12 years.

That's not a retirement. That's a few years of careful spending.

Path two. You increase your savings rate by 50 percent. Which, for most people in their 50s, means a significant lifestyle change. Maybe it's realistic, maybe it's not. Even then, you might get to $400,000 to $500,000 in real terms.

Better. Still not enough.

Path three. You change the vehicle.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "What if the problem isn't how much you're saving? What if it's WHERE you're putting it?"]

I'm not going to tell you what to invest in today. That's a different video. But I want you to sit with that question for a minute.

What if the problem was never the amount you saved? What if it was never about discipline or sacrifice or starting early enough?

What if the problem was the vehicle?

You've been driving a minivan in a Formula 1 race. And everyone around you keeps telling you to just press the gas harder.
