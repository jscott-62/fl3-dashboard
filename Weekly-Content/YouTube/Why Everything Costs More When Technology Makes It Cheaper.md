## SECTION 1: THE TWO FORCES (2:30 - 12:00)

### Part A: The Gift You Were Never Allowed to Receive (2:30 - 6:00)

Let me ask you something.

Have you ever stopped to think about how much cheaper things SHOULD be?

Not how much things cost. How much they SHOULD cost. If technology was allowed to do what technology does.

[GRAPHIC: Timeline showing technology cost reductions over 30 years: Computing -99%, Storage -99%, Communication -99%, Manufacturing -60%, Logistics -40%]

Look at this. Over the last 30 years, the cost of computing has dropped 99 percent. Storage? 99 percent. Communication? You can video call someone on the other side of the planet for free. Manufacturing costs are plummeting. Logistics are faster and cheaper than ever.

Technology is deflationary. That's not a theory. That's not an opinion. You've LIVED it. Every time you stream a movie instead of buying a DVD. Every time you use GPS instead of buying a paper map. Every time you deposit a check from your phone instead of driving to the bank.

Technology naturally pushes prices DOWN. It creates abundance. It makes things cheaper and more accessible for everyone.

That's the gift.

Now here's the question nobody asks.

If technology keeps making everything cheaper, where are those savings going? Because they're sure as hell not showing up in your grocery bill. Or your rent. Or your healthcare costs. Or your insurance premiums.

[GRAPHIC: Split screen. Left side: "Technology's promise: falling prices, more abundance." Right side: "Your reality: rising prices, shrinking purchasing power."]

Something is intercepting that gift before it reaches you.

And once you see what it is, you can't unsee it.

### Part B: What Jeff Booth Figured Out (6:00 - 9:00)

Jeff Booth is a tech entrepreneur who built one of Canada's largest tech companies. He spent decades watching technology drive costs down in every industry he touched. And then he looked up and noticed something that didn't make sense.

If technology is deflationary, and technology is accelerating faster than at any point in human history, why aren't prices falling?

[GRAPHIC: Jeff Booth quote on screen: "Prices aren't rising because things are scarce. Prices are rising because your money is."]

His answer is simple. And uncomfortable.

The problem isn't the economy. The problem is the money.

Here's what he means.

Our entire financial system runs on debt. Government debt. Corporate debt. Personal debt. The whole machine is built on borrowing.

And debt only works if money loses value over time. Think about that. If you borrow $100,000 today, you want to pay it back with dollars that are worth LESS in the future. That's how debt becomes manageable. That's how governments service their obligations. That's how the whole system stays afloat.

[GRAPHIC: Simple animation showing a dollar bill shrinking 2-3% per year over 20 years]

That shrinking? That's inflation. And it's not a natural law of economics. It's a policy choice. Governments and central banks engineer inflation because without it, the debt machine breaks down.

So here's the conflict.

Technology wants to make your life cheaper. The money system NEEDS to make it more expensive.

Two forces. Pulling in opposite directions.

Does that tension make sense to you? Because once you see it, everything clicks.

### Part C: The Tug of War You Were Never Told About (9:00 - 12:00)

Let me make this visual.

[GRAPHIC: Animated tug-of-war. Left side labeled "Technology: Deflation" pulling rope left (lower prices). Right side labeled "Monetary System: Inflation" pulling rope right (higher prices). A small figure labeled "Your Purchasing Power" stands in the middle.]

On one side, you have technology. Automation, software, AI, efficiency. All of it pushing costs down. Making goods cheaper. Making abundance possible.

On the other side, you have the monetary system. Debt, money printing, credit expansion. All of it pushing prices up. Making your dollar worth less every year.

These two forces are in direct conflict.

And here's what makes this personal for you.

Instead of letting technology pass those savings to you, the system prints more money, expands credit, and pushes prices back up. The deflationary gift gets swallowed by inflationary policy.

[GRAPHIC: Simple flow chart. "Technology reduces costs" → "Savings should flow to consumers" → "Central banks print money" → "Prices rise anyway" → "Your purchasing power shrinks"]

Your phone gets cheaper. But your groceries go up. Your streaming service costs less than cable ever did. But your rent doubles. Software is free. But healthcare will bankrupt you.

The technology savings are real. They're just being absorbed by a system that can't let prices fall.

Here's the part that really gets me.

Most people only ever learned about one side of this equation. The monetary side. The side that says inflation is normal. Inflation is healthy. Inflation is just how economies work.

Nobody taught you about the other side. The side that says technology should be making your life cheaper every single year.

You've been watching one half of a tug-of-war and wondering why you keep getting dragged backward.

Now you see the whole picture.

## SECTION 2: THE COVER STORY (12:00 - 19:00)

### Part A: Why You Were Never Taught This (12:00 - 14:30)

If this feels like new information, it's not your fault.

[GRAPHIC: Montage-style text building on screen: "Textbooks. Financial advisors. News anchors. Government reports. All repeating the same story."]

You grew up hearing that 2 percent inflation is healthy. That markets must always go up. That prices rising is just how economies work. Every textbook, every news anchor, every financial advisor reinforced the same message.

And here's the thing. They weren't lying. They were just repeating what they were taught, too.

Most economic models were built in an era where growth was tied to physical labor and physical capital. Factories. Machines. Raw materials. Those models assumed that productivity gains would be slow and linear.

They didn't account for exponential technological change. They didn't account for software eating the world. They didn't account for AI, automation, and digital abundance.

[GRAPHIC: Two curves on the same chart. Linear productivity growth (old model) vs. Exponential technology advancement (actual reality). The gap widens dramatically after 2000.]

The old models don't match the world we actually live in. But the old models are still what they teach in every economics class, every business school, every financial planning certification.

So when your advisor says "plan for 3 percent inflation," they're not trying to mislead you. They literally don't have a framework that accounts for the world Jeff Booth is describing.

And there's an emotional layer, too. Mainstream economics treats deflation, falling prices, as something dangerous. A threat. Something to be feared. So people are taught to believe that rising prices protect them.

They don't.

Rising prices protect the system. Not you.

### Part B: Deflation Is Not the Enemy (14:30 - 17:00)

I need to spend a minute on this because it's the biggest misconception standing between you and clarity.

[GRAPHIC: Big text on screen: "DEFLATION = FALLING PRICES. Why is that bad for YOU?"]

Think about what deflation actually means. Prices go down. Your dollar buys more. The money you've saved becomes more powerful over time, not less.

That sounds pretty good, right? Especially if you're someone who's been saving diligently for 30 years.

And yet you've been told your whole life that deflation is catastrophic. The Great Depression. Deflationary spirals. Economic collapse.

Here's what they leave out.

[GRAPHIC: Two columns. "Who deflation hurts: Governments with massive debt. Banks that profit from lending. Corporations leveraged to the hilt." vs. "Who deflation helps: Savers. Retirees. Fixed-income workers. Anyone holding cash."]

Deflation is only dangerous for people and institutions that are drowning in debt. Because when prices fall, the real value of debt increases. If you owe a million dollars and the dollar becomes more valuable, your debt just got heavier.

Governments can't have that. Banks can't have that. The whole system is built on debt staying manageable through inflation.

But for you? A saver? Someone trying to fund a retirement?

Deflation would be the best thing that ever happened to you.

Your savings would grow in purchasing power every year. Your cost of living would drop. The retirement number you need would shrink instead of expanding.

Technology should be delivering that to you right now. And it would be, if the monetary system allowed it.

But the system can't survive deflation. So instead of letting technology pass the savings to you, they print more money. They expand credit. They push prices back up.

And they call it "healthy."

Does that sound healthy to you?

### Part C: The Three Lines You Were Only Shown One Of (17:00 - 19:00)

Let me crystallize this.

[GRAPHIC: Three lines on the same chart over 20 years. Line 1: "Where prices SHOULD be" (declining, technology-driven deflation). Line 2: "Where prices ARE" (rising, inflation). Line 3: "Your savings growth" (barely keeping pace with Line 2)]

There are three lines that tell the whole story.

Line one is where prices should be. If technology was allowed to do its job, this line goes down. Your cost of living decreases. Your money becomes more powerful every year.

Line two is where prices actually are. Pushed up by monetary expansion, credit creation, and deliberate inflationary policy. This is the line you experience every time you go to the grocery store.

And line three? That's your savings. Growing at 7, maybe 10 percent a year if you're lucky. Barely keeping pace with line two. Nowhere close to the abundance that line one would have given you.

[GRAPHIC: Highlight the gap between Line 1 and Line 2, labeled "THE THEFT"]

That gap? Between where prices should be and where they actually are?

That's the theft.

Not from a hacker. Not from a scammer. From a system that intercepts the technological gift before it reaches your wallet.

Every year, technology generates trillions of dollars in efficiency gains. Those gains should be flowing directly to you. Better goods. Lower prices. More freedom.

Instead, they get absorbed by a machine that needs to keep inflating.

Your purchasing power erodes by design. Not by accident.

By design.

Here's the part most people find hardest to accept.

[GRAPHIC: Bold text on screen: "The system is not broken. It's working exactly as designed."]

The system isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed.

It's designed to transfer wealth from savers to debtors. From people who hold cash to people who hold assets. From people who follow the rules to people who understand how the game actually works.

[GRAPHIC: Simple wealth transfer diagram. Arrows showing purchasing power flowing FROM "Savers, retirees, wage earners" TO "Asset holders, borrowers, institutions"]

That's why saving money in a bank account feels pointless. Because it IS pointless, in the current system. Your savings lose purchasing power every year. On purpose. Not because the economy needs it. Because the debt load requires it.

That's why your 401(k) needs to return 7 percent just to keep up. That's why "safe" investments feel like they're going backward. That's why the traditional advice to park your money in bonds and wait feels like a trap.

Because it is one.
