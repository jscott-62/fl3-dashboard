## SECTION 1: THE INCENTIVE PROBLEM (2:00 - 6:30)

### Part A: How Your Advisor Actually Gets Paid (2:00 - 4:00)

Let's start with the money.

Most financial advisors use what's called an assets under management model. AUM for short. They charge a percentage of the total money you have invested with them. Usually about 1% per year.

[GRAPHIC: Simple illustration showing "$300,000 invested → 1% AUM fee → $3,000/year to advisor"]

So if you have $300,000 in your account, your advisor earns about $3,000 a year from you. If you have a million, they earn $10,000.

Sounds reasonable, right? They manage your money, they get a cut.

But think about what incentive that creates.

They don't get a bonus when your portfolio outperforms the market. They don't get paid extra when you reach your retirement goal faster. They get paid a percentage of whatever's in the account.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "They get paid when you STAY. Not when you WIN."]

Which means their primary financial incentive is to keep your money exactly where it is. Not to help you build wealth as fast as possible. Not to find you the best returns. To keep you parked.

Does that make sense to you? Because it changed everything for me.

### Part B: The Fee Stack You Don't See (4:00 - 6:30)

Now here's where it gets worse. The 1% advisor fee isn't the only fee you're paying.

[GRAPHIC: Fee stack diagram showing layers]

Your advisor puts your money into funds. Those funds have their own expense ratios. Usually another 0.3 to 0.8%. Let's call it 0.5%.

So now you're paying 1% to your advisor plus 0.5% in fund fees. That's 1.5% total.

Some accounts also have trading costs, administrative fees, 12b-1 marketing fees. I've seen total effective fees as high as 2.5% once you add everything up.

But let's be conservative. Let's say you're paying 1.5% all in.

[GRAPHIC: Math breakdown showing "7% gross return − 1.5% total fees = 5.5% net return"]

Your investment earns 7% (that's the historical average for a balanced portfolio). After fees, you're keeping 5.5%.

Now, that 5.5%, is it growing your wealth? Let's talk about that next. Because this is where the math gets ugly.

## SECTION 2: THE THREE MOVES (6:30 - 14:00)

### Part A: Move #1 - The Complexity Shield (6:30 - 9:00)

If you've been working with a traditional advisor for any length of time, you've experienced this.

They sit you down for the annual review. They pull up charts. They talk about asset allocation. Modern portfolio theory. Monte Carlo simulations. Risk-adjusted returns. Sharpe ratios. Tax-loss harvesting.

[GRAPHIC: Screen filling up with financial jargon, terms stacking on top of each other]

And here's the effect. You walk out feeling like investing is impossibly complicated. Like you could never figure this out on your own. Like you need them.

That's Move #1. I call it the Complexity Shield.

Now, I'm not saying these aren't real concepts. They are. Portfolio theory is real. Tax optimization matters. But when these concepts are deployed specifically to make you feel dependent, they become something else. They become a moat around the castle. Not to keep enemies out. To keep you in.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "Complexity Shield: Make investing feel impossible without them"]

And here's the part that hits home for a lot of people. If you're the kind of person who does their homework before making decisions, if you're someone who researches everything before acting, who has a folder of bookmarked articles and maybe some courses you haven't finished yet, this move is especially effective on you.

Because they give you just enough complexity to keep you in research mode forever. Always one more concept to understand. Always one more model to evaluate. Always one more meeting to schedule before you can make a decision.

Your thoroughness is a real strength. But the system has figured out how to turn it into a cage.

### Part B: Move #2 - The Risk Scare (9:00 - 11:30)

This one shows up every time you suggest doing something different.

"What about investing in real estate?" Too risky. Illiquid.
"What about this new asset class?" Too volatile. Unproven.
"What about managing some of my own money?" Too dangerous. You could lose everything.

[GRAPHIC: Shield with "TOO RISKY" stamped across it, blocking view of alternatives]

Move #2 is the Risk Scare. Everything outside their recommended portfolio gets the same label. Too risky.

They'll show you worst-case scenarios. Volatility charts. Horror stories from 2008 or the dot-com crash. Tales of people who lost everything chasing returns.

And you know what? Some of those stories are real. Some investments are genuinely risky. I'm not going to pretend otherwise.

But here's what they never, ever show you.

[POINT OF NO RETURN]

The worst-case scenario of their own advice.

[GRAPHIC: Side-by-side comparison. Left: "Their worst case for alternatives." Right: "The actual worst case of 'safe' 7% returns vs. real inflation"]

Let me show you the math.

Your balanced portfolio returns 7% on average. You pay 1.5% in fees. Your net return is 5.5%.

Now, real inflation. Not the government's CPI number. The actual cost-of-living increase you feel at the grocery store and the gas pump and the insurance bill. Conservative estimates put real inflation between 7 and 10% over the last two decades.

[GRAPHIC: Math line showing "5.5% net return − 8% real inflation = −2.5% purchasing power per year"]

At 5.5% returns and 8% real inflation, your purchasing power is shrinking by roughly 2.5% every single year.

You're not growing wealth. You're losing it. Slowly enough that the quarterly statement still looks green. But losing it.

That's the risk they never show you. The risk of doing exactly what they recommend.

### Part C: Move #3 - The Time Delay (11:30 - 14:00)

This is the one that costs you the most. Because time is the one thing you can't get back.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "Move #3: The Time Delay"]

"You still have time."
"Let's revisit this next quarter."
"The market will recover."
"Don't make any emotional decisions."

I've heard every single one of these. And sometimes, they're legitimate advice. Sometimes patience really is the right call.

But when your advisor says "you still have time" to a 52-year-old whose math isn't adding up, that's not patience. That's expensive.

[GRAPHIC: Calendar pages falling, with dollar signs disappearing on each one]

Here's why. Every quarter they delay a real conversation about your strategy, they collect another quarter of fees. Every year they tell you "let's stay the course," they earn another 1% on your assets.

The delay benefits them financially. Every time.

And for someone who already has a tendency to over-research and under-act, someone who wants to understand everything before making a move, this is devastating. Because the Time Delay weaponizes your natural caution.

You already lean toward "let me think about it more." They reinforce that lean. And another year goes by.

[GRAPHIC: Timeline showing age 50 → 52 → 54 → 56 with shrinking window of opportunity at each step]

I'm not saying you should make rash decisions. But there's a difference between being careful and being paralyzed. And the Time Delay is designed to keep you in paralysis while calling it prudence.

## SECTION 3: THEY'RE NOT BAD PEOPLE (14:00 - 16:30)

I want to pause here because this part matters.

I know how this sounds so far. Like I'm attacking financial advisors. Like I think they're all out to scam you.

I don't.

[GRAPHIC: Text overlay "They're not bad people. It's a bad playbook."]

Most financial advisors are genuinely good people. They got into the industry because they wanted to help families build security. They studied hard, passed their exams, and they do care about your outcomes.

The problem is the system they were trained inside.

The AUM model wasn't designed by villains. It was designed in the 1970s and 1980s when inflation was 4%, pensions still existed, and a balanced 60/40 portfolio could actually deliver enough growth to retire on.

That world is gone.

Inflation runs way beyond what the official numbers say. Pensions have disappeared. The 60/40 portfolio returns less than real inflation in many years. And new asset classes have emerged that the traditional system isn't built to offer you.

But the playbook hasn't been updated. The incentive structure hasn't changed. And the people running the playbook are too deep inside the system to see it from the outside.

This isn't about finding someone to blame. It's about recognizing that the game has changed and the rules you've been following haven't.

## SECTION 4: THE QUESTION THAT CHANGES EVERYTHING (16:30 - 18:30)

So what do you do with all this?

I'm not telling you to fire your advisor. I'm not saying go make a bunch of reckless moves. That's not what this is about.

I'm saying start with one question.

At your next meeting with your advisor, ask this:

[GRAPHIC: Full screen text - "What are the incentives behind this recommendation?"]

**"What are the incentives behind this recommendation?"**

That's it. One question.

Watch how they respond. Do they give you a straight answer? Do they get uncomfortable? Do they change the subject?

That response tells you everything you need to know about whether the advice you're getting is designed for your benefit or for theirs.

And the second step, the one that actually changes your trajectory, is educating yourself about what's working right now. Not what worked in 1985. Not theoretical models. What is actually generating real wealth for ordinary people who started late and needed to catch up fast.

Those vehicles exist. They're not hidden. They're not illegal. They're not even particularly complicated. They're just not what your advisor will ever recommend, because they sit outside the system that pays them.
